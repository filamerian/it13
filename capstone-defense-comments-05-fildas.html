Summary

The FilDAS project proposes a digital archiving and management system to solve inefficiencies in the QA office's document handling. The proposal is strong in identifying the problem and offering a system tailored to QA operations. The objectives, however, are written mostly as modules and should be rewritten to show outcomes and improvements. The methodology is appropriate, using Agile, but lacks details such as sprint length, evaluation standards, and database design. The review of literature is comprehensive but needs better synthesis and connection to the project's unique contributions.

General Problem

The Quality Assurance (QA) Office of Filamer Christian University faces inefficiencies in handling and managing documents due to reliance on manual processes, resulting in delays, disorganization, and lack of accountability.

These issues can be broken down into the following specific problems:

  1. Documents are uploaded and organized manually, making it difficult to ensure accuracy and consistency.

  2. Staff are not immediately informed when new documents are uploaded or updated, causing delays in coordination.

  3. Document sharing lacks access control, risking unauthorized access to sensitive QA information.

  4. Retrieving files is time-consuming because there is no efficient search and retrieval system.

  5. The QA office has no audit trail to monitor document-related activities, reducing accountability and transparency.

Scope & Limitations

The scope you presented is clear because it explains who the users are (e.g., QA staff, administrators) and what the system will do (archiving, notifications, access control, search, and audit logs). It shows the boundaries of your project and helps the reader know what is included.

However, this section can still be improved:

  1. Be more specific in the scope. List exactly which documents, users, and functions are covered in the system. For example, “The system will handle QA compliance documents and reports but will not cover student records or payroll files.”

  2. State the limitations in detail. Limitations should include things like required internet connection, hardware/software constraints, user training needs, or the fact that the project is only tested within the QA office.

  3. Separate scope and limitations clearly. Some parts mix the two. Write scope as what the system will do and limitations as what the system cannot do or the challenges it might face.

  4. Add boundaries on security and storage. For example, clarify if the system will include backup and recovery features or if that is outside your scope.

  5. Keep it realistic. Avoid over-promising features. Show what can really be done within your project timeline and resources.

  6. Mention ISO 21001 in the Scope. State that your system is designed to support document management practices in line with ISO 21001:2025 requirements, particularly in areas like transparency, accessibility of records, and continuous improvement.

    7. Example Scope sentence:
    “The system will be developed to align with ISO 21001:2025 by providing transparent, accessible, and reliable document management processes that support the QA office in ensuring compliance and continuous improvement.

  7. Clarify limitations regarding ISO compliance. Full ISO 21001 certification is not realistic within a capstone, since it requires institutional audits and long-term adoption. You should acknowledge that your project only demonstrates system features that can help the university in moving toward ISO compliance.

    Example Limitation sentence:
    “Although the system is designed following ISO 21001:2025 guidelines, full certification and implementation at the institutional level are outside the scope of this project."
Significance

Your section on the significance of the study is clear because it explains who will benefit (such as QA staff, administrators, and the university as a whole). It also shows that the system can improve efficiency, accuracy, and accountability.

To make this part stronger, you can:

  1. Be more specific about the benefits. Instead of general statements, describe exactly how each group benefits. For example, “QA staff will save time in retrieving files,” or “administrators will gain quicker access to compliance reports.”

  2. Add measurable impact. Mention possible improvements like reduced document retrieval time, fewer errors in archiving, or faster preparation of accreditation documents.

  3. Include long-term benefits. Connect the system’s impact to institutional goals, such as supporting ISO 21001:2025 compliance, accreditation, and continuous quality improvement.

  4. Use practical examples. For instance, explain how the system will help during audits or when preparing accreditation reports, making the benefits easier to visualize.

Additional notes

A university EDMS should not only store documents but also enhance accessibility, collaboration, accountability, and compliance, aligning with ISO 21001â€™s focus on quality management in educational organizations.

1. Document Capture & Input

  • Upload documents in multiple formats (PDF, Word, Excel, images)

  • Scan paper-based documents to digital format

  • Automated indexing based on metadata (author, date, department, document type)

  • Batch upload functionality for efficiency

2. Organization & Classification

  • Categorization by type: academic records, administrative forms, publications, policies

  • Departmental or program-based organization

  • Tagging and keyword assignment for easy search

  • Version control to track document revisions and updates

3. Access Control & Security

  • Role-based access (admin, faculty, staff, student)

  • Authentication (username/password, Single Sign-On if applicable)

  • Permission management for reading, editing, approving, or deleting documents

  • Audit trails for document access, modifications, and approvals

  • Data encryption and secure storage to ensure confidentiality

4. Search & Retrieval

  • Advanced search by keywords, author, date, department, or document type

  • Filters for faster retrieval (recent, popular, archived)

  • Full-text search for scanned or uploaded documents

  • Preview function without downloading

5. Workflow & Automation

  • Automated approval processes for policies, forms, and publications

  • Notifications and reminders for pending reviews or approvals

  • Alerts for document expiry, deadlines, or updates

  • Integration with email or learning management systems for communication

6. Collaboration & Feedback

  • Shared access for faculty and staff to collaborate on documents

  • Commenting or annotation system for document review

  • Feedback collection mechanisms for students, staff, or alumni (for publications, forms, or policies)

7. Reporting & Analytics

  • Track document usage (downloads, views, edits)

  • Generate reports on workflow status, pending approvals, or departmental activity

  • Monitor compliance with policies and ISO 21001 quality requirements

  • Insights for improving document management efficiency

8. Archiving & Retention

  • Long-term archival for important academic and administrative records

  • Retention schedule based on regulatory or institutional policy

  • Automatic backup and disaster recovery functionality

  • Easy retrieval of archived documents

9. Compliance & Standards

  • Ensure records management complies with ISO 21001:2025 and local educational regulations

  • Maintain quality documentation for auditing purposes

  • Support accessibility standards for users with disabilities (WCAG compliance)

  • Maintain traceability and accountability for all document actions

10. User Support & Training

  • Help guides and tutorials for users

  • Contextual tips within the system

  • Contact support for troubleshooting or technical assistance
