HSE-Archive: A Web-Based Repository for The Hillside Echo

Chapter I – Introduction

Evaluation:
The introduction explains the role of campus journalism, the importance of archiving school papers, and problems with physical records (loss, damage, inaccessibility). The rationale is convincing.

What to Revise:

  • Include baseline data: how many years/issues of the publication exist, how they are currently stored, and specific problems (e.g., missing issues, time to retrieve an article).
  • Shorten overly broad descriptions. Keep focus on the IT gap (lack of a structured, searchable archive).

Statement of the Problem

Evaluation:
Problems identified include lack of digital archiving, difficulties in accessing past issues, and risks of losing institutional memory.

What to Revise:

  • Make problems quantifiable: e.g., “In 40% of cases, requested past issues are unavailable or damaged.”
  • Explicitly note risks: lack of preservation, difficulty for alumni research, and limited accessibility.

Objectives

Evaluation:
Objectives include developing an online repository, digitizing issues, providing a search function, enabling access for students and alumni, and possibly adding extra features like alumni directories or chatbots.

What to Revise:

  • Refocus objectives: right now, they are too broad (archiving, alumni, chatbots, feedback).
  • Prioritize the core goal: archiving, categorization, and retrieval of school papers.
  • Write objectives in SMART format:
    • “Digitize at least 20 years of school paper issues within the project period.”
    • “Provide keyword-based search with at least 90% retrieval accuracy.”

Theoretical and Conceptual Framework

Evaluation:
The Task-Technology Fit (TTF) framework and conceptual diagrams are used. This is appropriate.

What to Revise:

  • Add technical diagrams:
    • ERD for the database design.
    • Use Case Diagram to show how admin, student, and alumni users interact with the system.
    • System Architecture Diagram showing how the web system is hosted, secured, and accessed.
  • Clarify in the conceptual framework how digitized files move from scanning to storage, indexing, and retrieval.
  • Indicate where security and access control apply.

Scope and Limitations

Evaluation:
Scope is defined: the system will focus on archiving the Hillside Echo publication.

What to Revise:

  • Narrow the scope by removing or postponing extra features (chatbot, alumni engagement). These can be “future work.”
  • Clearly state exclusions: the system does not cover general school documents, only the student publication.

Significance of the Study

Evaluation:
Explains benefits for students, alumni, administrators, and historians. Strong practical significance.

What to Revise:

  • Add academic/IT significance: contribution to information management systems, digital preservation, and applied Agile methodology.
  • Emphasize how this project aligns with digital transformation in education.

Chapter II – Review of Related Literature

Evaluation:
Covers related studies on library systems, repository platforms, and digital archiving. A comparison table is included.

What to Revise:

  • The review is mostly descriptive. It summarizes but doesn’t critique.
  • Organize into themes: (1) digital archiving, (2) library/repository systems, (3) access and search technologies, (4) usability and security in archives.
  • Add critical insights: what gaps did these systems fail to address? (e.g., lack of user-friendly search, poor metadata tagging, limited access control).
  • Strengthen sources by including peer-reviewed articles on digital repositories, archiving standards, and ISO practices.

Chapter III – Methodology

Evaluation:

Uses Agile methodology with phases: planning, design, coding, testing, deployment, and maintenance. Includes DFDs.
What to Revise:

  • Add a timeline (Gantt chart or sprint schedule) to demonstrate feasibility.
  • Assign team roles (frontend developer, backend developer, tester, documentation lead).
  • Expand testing plan:
    • Usability (System Usability Scale surveys).
    • Performance (document retrieval time).
    • Reliability (system uptime).
    • Security (access control checks, protection against SQL injection).
  • Add missing diagrams: ERD, Use Case, Deployment Architecture.

Prototype

Evaluation:
Prototype screenshots (if included) show login and interface.

What to Revise:

  • Add mockups for:
    • Uploading/scanning issues.
    • Search results page.
    • User access for alumni and students.
    • Administrative dashboard.
  • Improve design consistency and usability (clean UI, mobile responsiveness).

References

Evaluation:
References include local and foreign studies but lean heavily on descriptive sources.

What to Revise:

  • Ensure strict APA 7th formatting.
  • Add peer-reviewed journals on digital libraries, repositories, and archiving.
  • Reduce reliance on general websites.

Overall Assessment

Strengths:

  • Strong institutional relevance (preserves publication history).
  • Clear IT gap (manual archiving → digital repository).
  • Appropriate methodology (Agile).
  • Benefits are significant for both academic and cultural preservation.

Weaknesses:

  • Objectives are too broad, not fully SMART.
  • Literature review is descriptive, not analytical.
  • Missing technical diagrams (ERD, Use Case, System Architecture).
  • Testing metrics and feasibility timeline are not defined.
  • Prototype needs further development.

What to Revise (Summary):

  1. Strengthen problem with measurable data (number of issues, retrieval time).
  2. Narrow and rewrite objectives into SMART format.
  3. Add technical diagrams (ERD, Use Case, Architecture).
  4. Improve literature review with critique, thematic grouping, and journal sources.
  5. Add Gantt chart/timeline and assign team roles.
  6. Define clear evaluation metrics (usability, performance, reliability).
  7. Expand prototype mockups with more functions.
  8. Correct and strengthen APA references.
